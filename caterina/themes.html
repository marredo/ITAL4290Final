Caterina in the Big City themes

Coming of Age and Finding Your Own Voice

At the center of Caterina in the Big City is a very ordinary teen problem: figuring out who you are when everyone around you is trying to define you. Caterina arrives in Rome as a shy kid from "hillbilly country" and is suddenly pulled between stylish left-wing rebels and glossy right-wing rich kids. She tries on each identity for a while, but neither really fits. By the end, she stops letting groups, labels, or even her father's ambitions speak for her. Compared to A Chiara, where the stakes of growing up involve crime and physical danger, Caterina shows a quieter but very recognizable version of adolescence: learning to say "no" to people who want to use you and deciding who you want to become.

Class, Status, and "Being Someone"

The film constantly rubs Caterina's modest background against the wealth and connections of her classmates. Daniela's world of ministers, bodyguards, and lavish parties seems like a golden ticket out of Caterina's cramped apartment, while her father dreams of using those circles to finally become a "somebody." But the more Caterina moves between groups, the more she sees how closed those worlds really are. Both the bohemian left and the conservative right live comfortably and protect their own interests. Like A Chiara's Calabria or the bourgeois family in The Son's Room, Rome here is a place where class quietly decides who gets to feel secure and who has to hustle.

Politics as Teen Fashion (and Trap)

In Caterina's class, politics shows up as team colors: "communists" versus "fascists," rallies versus VIP weddings. Virzì plays this partly for comedy, but the point is sharp: even kids absorb Italy's polarized political climate and wear it like a brand. Margherita's crew uses radical talk and punk aesthetics; Daniela's circle waves through a wedding scene with a fascist salute. Caterina is less interested in ideology than in belonging, which makes her easy to pull in all directions. If A Chiara is about how organized crime shapes a teenager's choices, Caterina is about how organized politics and ideology try to do something similar, turning youth into symbols rather than listening to them.

Family Pressure and Generational Frustration

Caterina's father, Giancarlo, is one of the film's most important "problems." He's bitter, overeducated, stuck in a job he hates, and obsessed with finally being recognized. He pushes Caterina toward powerful people not because it's good for her, but because he hopes they'll open doors for him. Her mother, Agata, mostly absorbs his anger in silence. Through Caterina's eyes, we see a family where the parents haven't figured out adulthood but are still trying to steer their child. This connects nicely to The Son's Room: in both films, the parents' emotional limits shape how the teenager experiences the world, but Caterina lets us stay with the child's point of view as she quietly outgrows them.

Big City Culture and Everyday Teen Life

Finally, Caterina in the Big City is about how a place like Rome can overwhelm a teenager. Music, fashion, TV talk shows, protests, and expensive apartments all blur into Caterina's daily life. The city offers freedom and possibility, but also pressure to keep up and perform. She goes from choir and classical music to tattoos, protests, and VIP parties, then slowly back to a version of herself that includes what she actually loves. Where A Chiara ties youth culture to crime, and The Son's Room to grief and loss, Caterina focuses on the softer but constant noise of urban life—how a young girl learns to hear her own thoughts in the middle of it.
