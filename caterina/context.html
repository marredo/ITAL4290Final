Caterina in the Big City - Context

Paolo Virzì's Caterina va in città (Caterina in the Big City, 2003) follows a 13-year-old girl trying to figure out who she is in a city that already thinks it knows everything. After growing up in a small provincial town, Caterina moves to Rome with her parents: a quiet mother and a father who is a frustrated intellectual and aspiring writer. Suddenly she's at an elite Roman high school where the rules, accents, and social codes are all new, and where being "in" or "out" seems to matter more than anything else.

The world she lands in is split between rich classmates, sharp social boundaries, and two rival cliques: left-wing, bohemian kids who talk politics and poetry, and right-wing, TV- and celebrity-obsessed kids who move through glossy parties and media circles. For Caterina, all of this is confusing, seductive, and intimidating at the same time. We see Rome's politics and class divisions filtered through her eyes, while the adults around her—parents, teachers, media figures—often appear unstable, ridiculous, or hypocritical. The film captures what adolescence felt like in early-2000s Italy: pressure to choose a side, fit a certain image, and somehow break free from where you come from. Like Chiara and the teenage son in The Son's Room, Caterina has to navigate an adult world that suddenly looks nothing like the one she imagined growing up.
